Country Manners — Chapter 1 (Transcription)

The business of her life was to get her daughters married; its solace was visiting and news. But before we come to the family, a few words upon the neighbourhood and the season in which the story opens.

The village of Longbourn was only one mile from Meryton; a most convenient distance for the young ladies, who were usually tempted thither three or four times a week, to pay their duty to their aunt.

And to a milliner's shop just over the way. The two youngest of the family, Catherine and Lydia, were particularly frequent in these attentions; their minds were more vacant than their sisters'.

And when nothing better offered, a walk to Meryton was necessary to amuse their morning hours and furnish conversation for the evening; and however bare of news the country in general might be, they always contrived to learn some from their aunt.

At present, indeed, they were well supplied both with news and happiness by the recent arrival of a militia regiment in the neighbourhood; it was to remain the whole winter, and Meryton was the head quarters.

Their visits to Mrs. Philips were now productive of the most interesting intelligence. Every day added something to their knowledge of the officers' names and connections.

Their lodgings were not long a secret, and at length they began to know the officers themselves. Mr. Philips visited them all, and this opened to his nieces a source of felicity unknown before.

They could talk of nothing but officers; and Mr. Bingley's large fortune, the mention of which gave animation to their mother, was worthless in their eyes when opposed to the regimentals of an ensign.

It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune, must be in want of a wife.

However little known the feelings or views of such a man may be on his first entering a neighbourhood, this truth is so well fixed in the minds of the surrounding families, that he is considered as the rightful property of some one or other of their daughters.

The family of Bennet were the principal inhabitants of the village, and had been settled there for several generations upon an estate which, unhappily for the daughters, was entailed in default of heirs male.

Mr. Bennet was so odd a mixture of quick parts, sarcastic humour, reserve, and caprice, that the experience of three-and-twenty years had been insufficient to make his wife understand his character.

Her mind was less difficult to develop. She was a woman of mean understanding, little information, and uncertain temper. When she was discontented, she fancied herself nervous.

The solace of her life was visiting and news. The work of her life, as has been said, was to get her daughters well married, and to bring eligible young men within the orbit of the family.

Of the five daughters, Jane was the eldest and the most beautiful; Elizabeth, the second, had a quickness of observation and a playful disposition which delighted her father and tried her mother.

Mary was the only plain one in the family, and worked hard for knowledge and accomplishments; while Catherine and Lydia were given over wholly to the pursuit of officers and amusement.

It was upon such a household, settled in such a neighbourhood, that the great news of the season was now about to break, with consequences that the surrounding families could scarcely have foreseen.

However little known the feelings or views of such a man may be on his first entering a neighbourhood, this truth is so well fixed in the minds of the surrounding families, that he is considered the rightful property of some one or other of their daughters.

The neighbourhood was a populous one, and the families that composed it were of every degree of fortune and pretension, from the great house upon the hill to the modest parsonage beside the church.

Among them all, none watched the comings and goings of strangers with a keener interest than the inhabitants of Longbourn, where five unmarried daughters gave the question of new neighbours a peculiar urgency.

For a single man of large fortune, four or five thousand a year, was a fine thing for any of the girls; and the mere rumour of such a one settling in the vicinity was enough to set the whole house in a flutter.

Mrs. Bennet had long made it her study to anticipate such arrivals, and to be among the first to wait upon any family that promised a suitor for one of her daughters.

It was therefore with no small satisfaction that she received the intelligence which forms the proper beginning of this history, and which she communicated to her husband in the following manner.

She had been to Meryton that morning, and had there learned a piece of news which she could not keep to herself a moment longer than the dinner hour required.

Mr. Bennet, for his part, received her communications with that dry and provoking composure which he reserved for the occasions when his wife was most in earnest.

"My dear Mr. Bennet," said his lady to him one day, "have you heard that Netherfield Park is let at last?"

Mr. Bennet made no answer, but went on reading the newspaper which he held before him, as though he had not heard a single word that she had spoken.

"Do not you want to know who has taken it?" cried his wife impatiently, laying down her work and fixing her eyes upon him with an expression of great expectation.

"You want to tell me, and I have no objection to hearing it." This was invitation enough, and she proceeded to relate all that she had gathered upon the subject.

Mr. Bennet replied that he had not, and added, with the same composure, that he had no objection to her telling him whatever she pleased upon the matter.

"Why, my dear, you must know, Mrs. Long says that Netherfield is taken by a young man of large fortune from the north of England; that he came down on Monday in a chaise and four to see the place."

"And was so much delighted with it, that he agreed with Mr. Morris immediately; that he is to take possession before Michaelmas, and some of his servants are to be in the house by the end of next week."

"But it is," returned she; "for Mrs. Long has just been here, and she told me all about it."

Mr. Bennet wondered aloud whether the young man came with the design of settling among them, or merely for his own pleasure; a question which his wife dismissed as nonsensical.

"How can you be so tiresome! You must know that I am thinking of his marrying one of them. Design! nonsense, how can you talk so! But it is very likely that he may fall in love with one of them."

"And therefore you must visit him as soon as he comes." And with this declaration the conversation, for the present, was suffered to rest, though the subject was very far from being exhausted.
